# Sunk Cost Recovery Guide

A comprehensive guide to recognizing, understanding, and recovering from the sunk cost fallacy in business, investing, and everyday life.

## What Is the Sunk Cost Fallacy?

The sunk cost fallacy occurs when people continue a behavior or endeavor because of previously invested resources (time, money, effort) rather than evaluating the current and future value of continuing. This cognitive bias leads to irrational decision-making by making us feel that abandoning an effort would "waste" what we have already put in, even when continuing produces worse outcomes.

Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman and his colleague Amos Tversky demonstrated through extensive research that humans are particularly susceptible to this bias because of loss aversion -- we feel the pain of losses roughly twice as strongly as the pleasure of equivalent gains. This asymmetry creates a powerful psychological pull toward honoring past investments regardless of their relevance to future outcomes.

## Common Examples of Sunk Cost Fallacy

### In Business
- Continuing to fund a failing project because of the money already spent
- Keeping underperforming employees because of training investment
- Maintaining legacy software systems because of development costs
- Persisting with a marketing campaign that shows no returns

### In Personal Life
- Finishing a bad book because you already read half of it
- Staying in a bad relationship because of years invested
- Eating food you do not enjoy because you paid for it
- Continuing a degree program that no longer aligns with your goals

### In Investing
- Holding a declining stock because of the original purchase price
- Refusing to sell a property at a loss despite better opportunities elsewhere
- Doubling down on a failed investment strategy

## The Recovery Framework

### Step 1: Recognition
The first and most critical step is recognizing when the sunk cost fallacy is influencing your decisions. Ask yourself these questions:
- Would I make this same decision if I were starting from scratch today?
- Am I continuing because of past investment or future potential?
- What would I advise a friend in this exact situation?

### Step 2: Reframe the Decision
Separate past costs from future decisions. The money, time, or effort already spent is gone regardless of what you decide next. Focus exclusively on the marginal cost versus marginal benefit of continuing.

### Step 3: Establish Decision Rules
Create pre-commitment rules that trigger automatic reviews. For example:
- "If this project exceeds budget by 30%, we conduct a kill-or-continue review"
- "If this investment drops 20%, I reassess based on current fundamentals only"

Tools like [KeepRule](https://keeprule.com) can help you document and track these personal decision rules so they are available when emotions run high and rational thinking becomes difficult.

### Step 4: Seek External Perspective
People outside your situation are not emotionally invested and can offer clearer judgment. Build a network of trusted advisors who can challenge your reasoning.

### Step 5: Practice Deliberate Abandonment
Regularly review all ongoing commitments and actively choose to stop those that no longer serve your goals. Peter Drucker called this "planned abandonment" and considered it essential for organizational health.

## Psychological Mechanisms Behind Sunk Costs

Several cognitive mechanisms contribute to the sunk cost fallacy:

1. **Loss Aversion**: The tendency to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring equivalent gains
2. **Commitment Escalation**: Increasing commitment to a decision despite negative feedback
3. **Cognitive Dissonance**: The discomfort of acknowledging a past decision was wrong
4. **Endowment Effect**: Overvaluing things simply because we own or have invested in them
5. **Status Quo Bias**: Preferring the current state of affairs over change

## Practical Exercises

1. **The Clean Slate Test**: For every major ongoing commitment, ask "If I had not already started this, would I begin it today?"
2. **The Advisor Test**: Imagine advising someone else in your exact situation without your emotional attachment
3. **The Opportunity Cost Audit**: List what you could do with the resources currently tied up in questionable commitments
4. **The Pre-Commitment Contract**: Write down specific conditions under which you will abandon a course of action
